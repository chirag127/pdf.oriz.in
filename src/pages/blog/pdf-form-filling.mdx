## Types of PDF Forms

Not all PDF forms are created equal. Understanding the type of form you are dealing with determines how you can fill it out.

### Interactive (Fillable) Forms

Interactive PDF forms contain designated form fields: text boxes, checkboxes, radio buttons, dropdown menus, and signature fields. These fields are highlighted when you click them, and the cursor changes to indicate the field is active. Fillable forms are the most user-friendly type because the fields are designed for data entry.

### Flat (Non-Fillable) Forms

Flat forms are simply scanned documents or regular PDFs without embedded form fields. The text is either part of the page background or a scanned image. To fill out these forms, you need to add text annotations on top of the existing content, which is less precise but still functional.

### Hybrid Forms

Some PDFs mix interactive fields with flat content. Certain sections may have fillable fields while others require annotation-based filling. This is common in government forms that combine pre-printed information with user-entry areas.

## Handling Different Field Types

### Text Fields

Text fields accept typed input. Some have character limits, formatting requirements (phone numbers, dates), or validation rules (email format, numeric values). Pay attention to any placeholder text that indicates the expected format.

Common text field formats:
- **Name fields**: First name, last name, or full name
- **Date fields**: MM/DD/YYYY, DD-MM-YYYY, or calendar picker
- **Phone fields**: May auto-format as (XXX) XXX-XXXX
- **Address fields**: Sometimes split into street, city, state, zip
- **Currency fields**: Accept numeric input with decimal precision

### Checkboxes

Checkboxes are independent toggles. You can select zero, one, or multiple checkboxes in a group. Click to check, click again to uncheck. Checkboxes are commonly used for agreement statements, multiple-choice selections, and feature opt-ins.

### Radio Buttons

Radio buttons are mutually exclusive. Within a radio button group, selecting one option automatically deselects the previous selection. They are used when only one choice from a list is valid, such as selecting a gender, payment method, or yes/no answer.

### Dropdown Menus

Dropdown menus present a list of options in a compact form. Click the menu to expand it, then select your choice. Some dropdowns allow typing to filter the list, which is helpful when the options list is very long.

### Signature Fields

Signature fields allow you to draw, type, or insert an image of your signature. Some forms also support digital signatures that use cryptographic certificates for authentication.

<TipBox type="tip" title="Keyboard Shortcuts">
Use Tab to move to the next form field, Shift+Tab to move to the previous field, and Space to toggle checkboxes and radio buttons. These shortcuts significantly speed up form completion.
</TipBox>

## Saving and Sharing Completed Forms

### Saving Your Filled Form

After completing a PDF form, save it with a new filename that identifies the form and date. Use a naming convention like:

- `tax-return-2026-completed.pdf`
- `job-application-smith-company-2026.pdf`
- `medical-intake-john-doe-2026-02-08.pdf`

Saving with a descriptive name makes it easy to find and reference later.

### Choosing the Right Save Format

When saving a completed form, you have several options:

- **Save as PDF (with form data)**: Preserves the filled fields so the data can be edited later
- **Flatten the form**: Converts form fields to static content, preventing further edits. This ensures the data cannot be accidentally changed
- **Print to PDF**: Creates a new PDF that looks identical but has no form fields. Useful for submission where field editing is not desired

## Privacy and Security When Filling Forms Online

When using online tools to fill sensitive forms (tax documents, medical forms, legal contracts), privacy is a valid concern. Here are key considerations:

### Data Processing Location

Some online PDF tools process your files on their servers, meaning your data leaves your device. Others process entirely in your browser, keeping your data local. For sensitive forms, prefer browser-based processing that never uploads your data.

### Encryption and Secure Connections

Always use tools that serve over HTTPS (the padlock icon in your browser). This encrypts the connection between your browser and the server, preventing interception of your data during upload.

### Data Retention Policies

Check whether the tool retains your files after processing. Reputable tools delete uploaded files automatically after a short period (typically one hour or less). For the most sensitive documents, use tools that process locally and never upload your data at all.

### Form Data Sensitivity

Consider the sensitivity of the data you are entering:
- **Low sensitivity**: Contact information, general preferences
- **Medium sensitivity**: Employment history, financial information
- **High sensitivity**: Social Security numbers, health information, bank account details

For high-sensitivity forms, use offline tools or browser-based processing only.

<TipBox type="warning" title="Security Reminder">
Never fill out forms containing Social Security numbers, bank account details, or health information on untrusted websites. For sensitive forms, use local PDF software or tools that process entirely in your browser without uploading files.
</TipBox>

## Troubleshooting Common Form Issues

### Form Fields Not Responding

If clicking on a form field does not activate it, the PDF may be a flat (non-fillable) form. Check for interactive fields by hovering your mouse over the form area—the cursor should change to a text insertion cursor or I-beam when over an active field.

### Unable to Save Form Data

Some PDF viewers do not save form data when you close the file. Always use "Save As" rather than "Save" to ensure the filled data is preserved. Online tools typically prompt you to download the completed form.

### Checkboxes or Radio Buttons Not Working

Some older PDFs use non-standard form field implementations that may not work in all viewers. Try a different PDF tool or browser if specific fields do not respond to clicks.

### Form Looks Different After Saving

If the saved form looks different from the on-screen version, it may be due to font embedding issues. Some fields use system fonts that are not embedded in the PDF. When the form is opened on a different system, substituted fonts may alter the layout.
