## Understanding Page Orientation

Page orientation refers to the direction in which a page is oriented. The two standard orientations are portrait and landscape, and the choice between them depends on the type of content you are presenting.

### Portrait Orientation

Portrait orientation is taller than it is wide, matching the natural orientation of most printed documents. It is the default for letters, reports, books, essays, and most business documents. Portrait layout works best for text-heavy content, narrow tables, and vertically-oriented images.

### Landscape Orientation

Landscape orientation is wider than it is tall. It is ideal for wide tables, charts, diagrams, presentations, photographs, and any content that benefits from a horizontal format. Landscape is also commonly used for certificates, calendars, and spreadsheets.

## Standard Paper Sizes

Different regions and industries use different standard paper sizes. Understanding these standards ensures your PDFs print correctly on any printer worldwide.

### ISO 216 (A Series)

The ISO 216 standard is used in most countries outside of North America. The A series is based on a consistent aspect ratio of 1:√2 (approximately 1:1.414). This mathematical relationship means that scaling between sizes is seamless—two A4 pages side by side equal one A3 page.

**Common ISO A sizes:**
- **A0**: 841 × 1189 mm — posters, architectural drawings
- **A1**: 594 × 841 mm — large charts, technical drawings
- **A2**: 420 × 594 mm — medium posters, diagrams
- **A3**: 297 × 420 mm — spreadsheets, small posters
- **A4**: 210 × 297 mm — standard documents, letters
- **A5**: 148 × 210 mm — booklets, notepads
- **A6**: 105 × 148 mm — postcards, pocket notebooks

### North American Sizes

The United States, Canada, and parts of Mexico use a different set of paper sizes based on inches rather than millimeters.

**Common North American sizes:**
- **Letter**: 8.5 × 11 inches — standard US document size
- **Legal**: 8.5 × 14 inches — contracts, legal documents
- **Tabloid/ Ledger**: 11 × 17 inches — newsletters, large tables
- **Executive**: 7.25 × 10.5 inches — personal documents, stationery

### Industry-Specific Sizes

Some industries use specialized sizes:

- **Business Card**: 3.5 × 2 inches (US) or 85 × 55 mm (ISO)
- **Envelope #10**: 4.125 × 9.5 inches
- **Photo 4×6**: 4 × 6 inches
- **Photo 5×7**: 5 × 7 inches

## Custom Page Sizes

Not every document fits a standard page size. Custom sizes are essential for:

- **Marketing materials**: Postcards, flyers, and brochures in non-standard dimensions
- **Digital displays**: Documents optimized for specific screen sizes
- **Packaging**: Product inserts, labels, and box designs
- **Art prints**: Custom dimensions matching the artwork's aspect ratio
- **Banners and signs**: Very wide or very tall formats

### Setting Custom Dimensions

When creating custom-sized PDFs, specify dimensions in either millimeters or inches. Common custom sizes include:

- **DL (Dimension Lengthwise)**: 99 × 210 mm — fits a DL envelope, used for flyers and invitations
- **B5**: 176 × 250 mm — intermediate size between A4 and A5, used for books
- **Half Letter**: 5.5 × 8.5 inches — US half-sheet format
- **Statement**: 5.5 × 8.5 inches — same as Half Letter

<TipBox type="tip" title="Aspect Ratio Matters">
When creating custom page sizes, consider the aspect ratio. The ISO 216 standard's 1:√2 ratio ensures consistent scaling. Custom ratios may require manual adjustment when scaling content between sizes.
</TipBox>

## Multi-Layout Documents

Many professional documents use multiple page layouts within a single PDF. A report might have portrait text pages interspersed with landscape charts, or a presentation might combine slides with speaker notes on differently-sized pages.

### Mixing Orientations

Modern PDF supports different orientations on different pages within the same document. This is particularly useful for:

- Annual reports with portrait text sections and landscape financial tables
- Technical manuals with portrait instructions and landscape diagrams
- Photo books with alternating portrait and landscape photographs

### How PDF Stores Page Dimensions

Each page in a PDF has its own MediaBox, which defines the dimensions of the page. PDF also supports additional box definitions:

- **MediaBox**: The full page size, including all content and margins
- **CropBox**: The visible area when the page is displayed or printed
- **BleedBox**: The area that extends beyond the trim edge for professional printing
- **TrimBox**: The final intended page size after trimming
- **ArtBox**: The area containing the meaningful content

## Page Layout for Print vs. Screen

The intended output medium significantly affects your page layout choices.

### Print-Optimized Layouts

For documents destined for the printer:

- Use standard paper sizes (A4, Letter) to ensure compatibility with all printers
- Include 3mm bleed for documents with edge-to-edge color or images
- Set appropriate margins to avoid content being cut off during printing
- Use CMYK color space for accurate color reproduction
- Target 300 DPI resolution for crisp output

### Screen-Optimized Layouts

For documents that will only be viewed on screens:

- Consider widescreen aspect ratios (16:9, 16:10) for presentations
- Use smaller page sizes to reduce file size and scrolling
- Optimize for 150 DPI since screens cannot display higher resolution
- Use RGB color space for vibrant on-screen colors
- Consider responsive layouts that adapt to different screen sizes

## Changing Page Layouts in Existing PDFs

Sometimes you need to change the layout of an existing PDF—rotating pages, resizing, or adjusting margins. Here are the common operations:

### Rotating Pages

Page rotation changes the orientation of individual pages without altering the content. PDF supports 90°, 180°, and 270° rotations. Rotating a portrait page 90° clockwise transforms it into landscape orientation.

### Resizing Pages

Changing page dimensions requires scaling or cropping the content to fit the new size. This operation may affect text readability and image quality if significant size changes are involved.

### Adjusting Margins

Some PDF tools allow you to change the visible page area (CropBox) without altering the underlying content. This effectively adjusts margins without reflowing text or rescaling images.

## Accessibility Considerations

Page layout affects document accessibility. Large page sizes may require horizontal scrolling on assistive devices, and unusual aspect ratios can cause navigation difficulties.

### Accessibility Best Practices

- Use standard page sizes to ensure compatibility with screen readers
- Maintain consistent page dimensions throughout the document
- Use sufficient font sizes (minimum 12pt for body text)
- Ensure adequate contrast between text and background
- Tag the PDF structure for assistive technology navigation
